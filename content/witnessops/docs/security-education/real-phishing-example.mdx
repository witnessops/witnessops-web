# A Real Phishing Email

## Problem this page solves

Phishing examples often become stories instead of investigations, which makes response decisions drift away from evidence.
This page shows a representative phishing artifact set and ties each conclusion to what is directly observable.

## Reader outcome

After this page, you should be able to:

- walk a phishing report through delivery, interaction, credential, and post-access stages
- state what each artifact supports and what it does not support
- choose a response gate that matches the evidence quality

## Mechanism-first walkthrough of a representative phishing artifact or event

This walkthrough is synthetic but mechanically representative.
Treat it as an artifact-handling exercise, not as attribution.

### Representative lure artifact (preserved message excerpt)

```txt
From: Microsoft Account Team <security@microsft-account.com>
Reply-To: noreply@microsft-account.com
Subject: Unusual sign-in activity on your account

We detected unusual activity.
Verify your account now to avoid access restrictions.

Verify My Account:
https://login-microsoft-security-check[.]com/verify
```

### Stage-by-stage artifact walkthrough

| Chain stage | Primary artifact | What to verify in that artifact | Evidence boundary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Delivery | `phishing-email.eml` | sender or reply-to mismatch, header path, auth results | supports suspicious-message classification, not account compromise |
| Interaction | click telemetry or browser history | whether and when the user opened or clicked | supports interaction timing, not credential exposure |
| Credential capture | fake-page capture + user statement | non-canonical login domain, credential entry claim, unexpected MFA prompt context | supports likely credential exposure when corroborated |
| Session establishment | identity sign-in logs | successful sign-in from an unfamiliar IP or device after interaction | supports possible unauthorized session |
| Post-access actions | mailbox or cloud audit logs | forwarding rules, outbound anomalies, token or app changes | supports impact progression if present |

## Observable indicators and what each indicator supports

| Observable indicator | Source artifact | What it supports | What it does not prove by itself |
|---|---|---|---|
| Claimed brand does not match sender domain | preserved email headers | impersonation attempt | that credentials were entered |
| Link resolves to a non-canonical or lookalike domain | resolved URL evidence | lure destination is attacker-controlled or untrusted | that compromise already occurred |
| User confirms entering credentials or approving MFA | user interview + timeline note | account-exposure likelihood | that the attacker successfully authenticated |
| Successful new sign-in after the interaction window | identity-provider sign-in logs | possible unauthorized session establishment | full blast radius |
| New forwarding rule or unusual outbound activity | mailbox audit logs | post-access activity likely occurred | attacker intent or attribution |

## Response decisions and escalation gates tied to evidence quality

- **Gate A (exposure-only):** preserved lure artifact, no click or open evidence.
  - Decision: classify exposure-only, block indicators, notify the user, document the receipt.
- **Gate B (interaction):** click or open evidence exists, but no credential, MFA, or session evidence.
  - Decision: preserve the full timeline, monitor sign-in telemetry, run endpoint checks if attachment interaction occurred.
- **Gate C (account exposure likely):** credential entry, MFA approval, or suspicious new session evidence.
  - Decision: reset credentials, revoke sessions or tokens, inspect mailbox changes, execute [Phishing Investigation](/docs/scenarios/phishing-investigation).
- **Gate D (high impact):** privileged account, multi-user spread, or payment/workflow impact.
  - Decision: escalate through the governed incident path with [Policy Gates](/docs/security-systems/policy-gates) before high-impact actions.

## What WitnessOps controls support vs cannot guarantee

**WitnessOps controls can support:**

- [Phishing Investigation](/docs/scenarios/phishing-investigation) for governed triage and containment sequencing
- [What Evidence Is Required?](/docs/decisions/evidence-required) for minimum evidence completeness before intrusive action
- [Do I Need to Escalate?](/docs/decisions/escalation) for escalation criteria tied to impact and uncertainty
- [Sensitive Artifact Handling](/docs/evidence/sensitive-artifact-handling) for controlled handling of email, URL, and identity artifacts
- [Receipts](/docs/evidence/receipts) for signed, replayable decision records

**WitnessOps cannot guarantee:**

- prevention of all phishing delivery attempts
- complete reconstruction when telemetry is missing, delayed, or altered
- attacker attribution from a single lure or sign-in event
- external recovery outcomes, such as third-party financial reversal

## Observed vs inferred separation

- **Observed:** preserved email headers, resolved URL, click timestamp, sign-in event record, mailbox rule-change log.
- **Inferred:** "credentials were definitely stolen," "the attacker fully controlled the mailbox," or "scope is complete."

Use observed artifacts to justify immediate containment.
Keep inferred claims explicit and provisional until more evidence confirms them.

## Trust assumptions and limits

Assumptions:

- email, identity, and mailbox telemetry is retained and time-aligned enough to reconstruct sequence
- original artifacts are preserved before mailbox cleanup or endpoint remediation
- high-impact actions follow policy and approval gates

Limits:

- delayed reporting can remove short-lived evidence, such as redirect chains or transient sessions
- adversaries may evade, suppress, or overwrite telemetry
- some scope conclusions remain probabilistic until more corroborating evidence is collected
