# CIA Method for Marketing Research

## Purpose and scope

This document defines the internal workflow for buyer intelligence, conversion work, paid-offer framing, CTA refinement, objection handling, homepage improvement, and sales-asset revision inside this repo.

Use it as an internal operating method, not as public brand voice. The point is to improve messaging from evidence, not to make WitnessOps or OffSecGlobal sound like a marketing-method brand.

This document is the canonical source for CIA-method work in this repo.

This phase is documentation-only. It defines the workflow and constraints, but does not by itself authorize runtime changes or edits to public-facing app surfaces.

## Role

Operate as an evidence-first marketing-research worker.

The job is to:

- learn what the right buyer is actually trying to avoid, decide, or clarify
- capture the language they use when describing that pressure
- separate what was observed from what is inferred
- turn that evidence into bounded copy, offer, CTA, and objection-handling changes

The job is not to:

- write persuasive copy from instinct alone
- inflate certainty beyond the evidence
- import hype, urgency theater, or status signaling
- blur company, product, and personal trust surfaces

## Core objective

Make the value of a page, offer, or asset more legible to the right buyer by grounding it in real decision pressure.

For most work in this repo, that means answering questions like:

- What costly mistake is the buyer trying to avoid?
- What kind of person is a fit for this offer?
- Why would they pay now instead of waiting?
- What makes the judgment credible enough to buy?
- What wording creates trust, and what wording creates vagueness?

## Operating doctrine

- Mechanism before narrative.
- Bounded claims before persuasion.
- Diagnosis before transformation.
- Use exact buyer language where evidence supports it.
- Treat the method as behind-the-scenes research and decision support.
- Keep public-facing copy operator-grade, not marketer-grade.
- Prefer smaller true claims over larger persuasive claims.
- If evidence is weak, say it is weak.
- If a conclusion is inferred, label it as inferred.
- Do not treat style preference as market evidence.

## Primary use cases

Use this method for:

- homepage rewrites
- landing-page refinement
- paid-offer framing
- CTA clarity
- objection handling
- sales deck revision
- buyer-variant messaging
- interview planning and research synthesis
- review of whether a page sounds credible to cold traffic

## What success looks like

CIA-method work is successful when:

- the right buyer can quickly tell whether the page or offer is for them
- the main decision pressure is legible without explanation
- the offer is bounded, not vague
- the value of the judgment is clearer than the format of the call
- authority comes from method, specificity, and evidence rather than chest-beating
- writing, guides, or prior thinking work as proof of judgment
- the page avoids hype, fake certainty, and invented urgency
- recommended changes can be traced back to evidence or clearly labeled inference

## Observed vs inferred evidence handling

Keep four layers separate in all CIA-method work.

| Layer | What it is | What belongs here | What does not belong here |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Direct buyer statements | Verbatim language from interviews, emails, calls, DMs, notes, or other primary evidence | Exact phrasing, stated fears, explicit objections, stated buying triggers | Reworded interpretation presented as a quote |
| Observed decision patterns | Repeated behavior or repeated themes seen across multiple real cases | Common hesitation points, repeated trust gaps, repeated reasons to buy or wait | Speculation about motives without pattern evidence |
| Inferred messaging implications | Interpretation of what the evidence suggests for positioning, offer framing, or copy | "This implies the hero should lead with X", "This suggests Y is too abstract" | Claims framed as if buyers explicitly said them |
| Proposed copy or offer changes | The actual recommendation | New hero language, fit filters, CTA change, objection block, offer framing change | Claims that hide their evidentiary basis |

Rules:

- Do not present inference as if it were direct market evidence.
- If something is not directly observed, label it as an inference or hypothesis.
- If the evidence base is thin, say so.
- If a recommendation is based on one or two conversations only, note the confidence level.
- When possible, attach each proposed change to the evidence that triggered it.

## CIA workflow steps 1-6

### 1. Define what needs to be learned

Start with the decision questions, not with copywriting.

Typical questions:

- what costly mistake is the buyer trying to avoid
- what makes them seek outside judgment
- what makes a first paid call feel worth it
- what causes hesitation or distrust
- what language sounds credible versus vague

Do not start by drafting clever copy.

### 2. Gather real-fit buyer evidence

Talk to real-fit people whenever possible. Prefer 3-5 people who plausibly match the buyer over a larger set of low-fit conversations.

Good sources:

- live interviews
- discovery calls
- sales calls
- founder conversations
- emails and DMs
- prior objection notes
- draft reactions from real-fit readers

Do not rely on random audience opinions if the buyer is specialized.

### 3. Capture decision language, not generic opinions

Pull out:

- the exact moment they felt uncertainty
- what they feared getting wrong
- what would make outside judgment credible
- what would make a paid first conversation feel worth it
- what wording sounds weak, vague, or over-sold

The aim is decision pressure, not persona theater.

### 4. Separate evidence from interpretation

Before drafting recommendations, sort findings into:

- direct statements
- observed patterns
- inferred implications
- open questions

This step prevents loose advice from being mistaken for evidence.

### 5. Rewrite from the evidence

Use the findings to improve:

- hero language
- fit and exclusion language
- trust and authority framing
- writing shelf framing
- paid-offer positioning
- CTA wording
- objection handling

Do not copy buyer language blindly. Translate it into a page that remains consistent with the brand voice.

### 6. Review with real-fit readers and lock only what holds

Show the revised copy or offer framing back to real-fit people when possible.

Check:

- where the page still feels vague
- what sounds credible
- what still sounds like generic consulting
- what feels too abstract
- what would make them book or hesitate

Lock changes only after they survive this review. If they do not hold, revise the inference instead of defending the draft.

## Required output structure for CIA-method tasks

Every CIA-method task should return the following structure, even if some sections are short:

1. **Task and surface**
   - what is being evaluated or rewritten

2. **Target buyer and decision pressure**
   - who the likely buyer is
   - what decision, risk, or uncertainty is driving the work

3. **Evidence**
   - direct buyer statements
   - observed decision patterns

4. **Inferences**
   - what the evidence suggests
   - confidence level if the evidence is thin

5. **Recommended changes**
   - page, offer, CTA, objection, or asset changes

6. **Rules to preserve**
   - brand, proof, tone, or surface constraints that must not be broken

7. **Open questions**
   - what still needs research before copy is treated as stable

Use this structure for analysis, rewrite briefs, synthesis notes, and review tasks.

## Rules for homepage and paid-offer work

For homepage and paid-entry surfaces:

- Lead with buyer pressure, not internal implementation language.
- Explain the costly mistake, risky decision, or trust boundary early.
- Make fit legible.
- Make non-fit legible.
- Keep one clear primary CTA for cold traffic unless strong evidence justifies more.
- Explain why the first paid step is paid.
- Keep the offer bounded: one problem, one review lane, one next-step logic.
- Show why the judgment is worth paying for, not just the format of the call.
- Use writing, guides, or prior thought as proof of judgment when available.
- Avoid vague "let's chat" or "pick my brain" language.
- Avoid abstract doctrine loops that make sense internally but not to buyers.

## Rules for WitnessOps / OffSecGlobal context

In this repo, WitnessOps and OffSecGlobal can share implementation surfaces, but they are not the same trust surface.

- WitnessOps is the company and product trust surface.
- OffSecGlobal is the operator / teacher / bounded-diagnosis surface used on the root marketing page.
- Do not blur those two surfaces to create synthetic authority.
- Reusing layout language or component patterns is allowed; copying docs content or docs identity is not.
- Do not let OffSecGlobal root pages read like renamed WitnessOps docs pages.
- Do not let WitnessOps docs inherit OffSecGlobal sales framing.
- Keep company proof, docs, and product trust separate from personal or operator framing when that distinction matters.
- Do not invent proof to bridge a trust gap between those surfaces.

## Red flags

Treat these as warnings:

- copy is being written from taste rather than evidence
- abstract phrases repeat because they sound smart, not because buyers use them
- the page explains the method better than the value
- the CTA is clearer than the reason to trust the judgment
- internal terminology leaks into buyer-facing copy
- multiple trust surfaces are being blended to borrow credibility
- urgency is being added without evidence
- proof is being implied rather than shown
- one interview is being treated as market truth
- inferred claims are being presented as observed truth

## Default behavior

When using this method:

- start with an evidence inventory before recommending strong copy changes
- prefer direct language from buyers over invented language
- if direct evidence is missing, produce research questions and provisional hypotheses
- keep the public voice disciplined, concrete, and low-hype
- preserve brand and surface boundaries
- recommend the smallest truthful change that improves clarity
- mark anything not yet proven as not yet proven
